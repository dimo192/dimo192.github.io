Simulation of Magnetic diffusion eqaution

Abstract

The simulation of galactic mean-field dynamos serves as a method in understanding the dynamics of cosmic magnetic fields. By simulating a mean field dynamo numerically we gain an understanding of conditions required the generation and decay of different modes of magnetic field in cosmic systems.

In this particular example we explore the relationship between the motion of the fluid and the existing magnetic field of the system(The relationship for which is given by the \(\alpha\)-term). By doing this we can explore if any and every form of interaction between the field and the fluid will lead to an exponential growth of the dynamo.

In our code we use 4th order finite differencing and 6th order Runge kutta for simulating the dynamo and getting the results. We see that while the \(\alpha\)-term plays a major role in the form of the final magnetic field and the time it takes to reach this value.

As cosmic events take place over the scale of gigayears this implies that the \(\alpha\)-term plays a major role in determining if the dynamo is a fast or slow dynamo.

Introduction

The mean field dynamo equation is given by

\(\frac{\partial \overline{\boldsymbol{B}}}{\partial t} = \nabla \times (\overline{\boldsymbol{V}} \times \overline{\boldsymbol{B}} + \mathcal{E} - \eta \nabla \times \overline{\boldsymbol{B}})\) ` (1)

where, \(\mathcal{E} = \alpha \overline{\boldsymbol{B}} - \eta_{\mathrm{t}} \boldsymbol{\nabla} \times \overline{\boldsymbol{B}}\) and ignore the induction term \(\nabla \times (\overline{\boldsymbol{V}} \times \overline{\boldsymbol{B}})\). This can symplify the equation to

\(\frac{\partial \overline{\boldsymbol{B}}}{\partial t} = \nabla \times (\alpha \overline{\boldsymbol{B}} - (\eta_{\mathrm{t}}+\eta) \boldsymbol{\nabla} \times \overline{\boldsymbol{B}} )\) (2)

Changing to cylinderical co-ordinates and assuming axis symmetric solution \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \phi}\)=0; and \(\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\)=0 due to slab approximation.

\(\frac{\partial \overline{\boldsymbol{B_r}}}{\partial \tilde{t}} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial \tilde{z}}(\tilde{\alpha}\overline{B_\phi})+\frac{\partial^2}{\partial z^2}(\overline{B_r})\) (3)

\(\frac{\partial \overline{\boldsymbol{B_\phi}}}{\partial \tilde{t}} = -q\tilde{\Omega}\overline{B_r}+\frac{\partial^2}{\partial z^2}(\overline{B_\phi})\) (4)

As can be seen in equations (3) and (4), we see that only equation (3) is affected by the \(\alpha\)-term. This term is the advecture term and causes the dynamo to grow over time.

Methods and Code

In this simulation we employ 4th order finite differencing for spacial derivatives and 6th order runge kutta for time derivative[1]. We follow the following steps

Algorithm

  1. Step 1: Start with initialising a grid with choice number of grid points.
  2. Step 2: Initialise two lists that will store the variables of magnetic field along r and another along \(\phi\).
  3. Step 3: Start at t=0 and increase t till a \(T_{max}\) is aciheved, do the following substeps for each t
    1. Substep A: Calculate the space derivative of B at time t(Used 6th order finite differencing). Calculate the evolution of the magnetic field at time t+1 using the spacial derivatives(Rk6 used).
    2. Substep B: Check if norm B has a magnitude lower than the maximum allowe magnetic field. Update magnetic field along r and \(\phi\) only if it is lower.
    3. Substep C: Save these in 2 seperate 2d arrays to be used for calculation later.
  4. Step 4: When t=\(T_{max}\) calculate \(\gamma\).
An additional constrain of maximum allower magnetic field was introduced to ensure that the dynamo reaches stauration within a finite time which is computationally feasible.

By doing this we can compare the time it takes for the dynamo with specific parameters to reach saturation magnetic field and compare then for the same dynamo number.

Some specific cases that is of particular interest is

  1. \(\alpha\)=0; this leads to critical dynamo number 0 but it will interesting to see if the dynamo every grows, as \(\Omega\) term which is advecture for \(B_\phi\) still is non zero.
  2. \(\alpha=\pm c\); this will compare if the sign of \(\alpha\) matters and what happens to the saturation time in this case.
  3. \(\alpha=sin(\pi \frac{z}{nh})\); where z is distance of measurement from midplane, n is a natural number. By comparing for variuos n we can see if there is any effect on the saturation time.
  4. \(\alpha=\pm\frac{z}{nh}\); where n is a natural number. By comparing for variuos n we can see if there is any effect on the saturation time.

Discussion

The simulation was done sucesfully and it was observed that while \(\alpha\)-term does play a major role in deteimining if the dynamo is fast or slow it does not cause a dynamo to decay. This is expected from the equations as

\(\frac{\partial \overline{\boldsymbol{B_r}}}{\partial \tilde{t}} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial \tilde{z}}(\tilde{\alpha}\overline{B_\phi})+\frac{\partial^2}{\partial z^2}(\overline{B_r})\)

\(\frac{\partial \overline{\boldsymbol{B_\phi}}}{\partial \tilde{t}} = -q\tilde{\Omega}\overline{B_r}+\frac{\partial^2}{\partial z^2}(\overline{B_\phi})\)

We see that \(\frac{\partial \overline{\boldsymbol{B_r}}}{\partial \tilde{t}}\) has a term that depends on \(\alpha\) but no other term does so. Thus as discussed in the case when \(\alpha\)=0 throughout the system; we observe that the growth \(\boldsymbol{B_\phi}\) is not affected by \(\alpha\) and the \(\boldsymbol{B_\phi}\) grows exponentially under centrain cunditions(dynamo number greater than critical dynamo number) and as \(\boldsymbol{B_r}\) has a term that involves gradient square of \(\boldsymbol{B_\phi}\). As expected we observed that \(\boldsymbol{B_r}\) also grows slowly. Thus the dynamo takes longer time to reach the maximum magnetic field.
